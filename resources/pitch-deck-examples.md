## The frameworks

### Sequoia's classic 10-slide structure

The original. Still works.

1. Company purpose — one sentence
2. Problem — whose pain, how big
3. Solution — the value-prop in plain English
4. Why now — what's changed in the world
5. Market size — TAM/SAM/SOM (be honest)
6. Competition — who else, why you win
7. Product — screenshots, demo
8. Business model — pricing, unit economics
9. Team — why you, why now
10. Financials — historical and projected

### YC's "founder-friendly" structure

Simpler. The version YC partners actually use in office hours.

1. What does your company do?
2. What's the size of the market?
3. What's your unique insight?
4. How are you doing? (traction)
5. What's your unique advantage?
6. What's your business model?
7. Who's the team?
8. How much are you raising?

### Mike Maples' "minimum viable pitch"

For first conversations, before the formal deck.

- Mission
- Insight (what do we know that nobody else does?)
- Magic (the demo / "how it works" moment)
- Method (the unique mechanism)
- Math (numbers)
- Mafia (team)
- Money (the ask)

### The 3-slide version (use it when an investor texts you "send me something")

1. **What you do** + best customer screenshot or demo gif
2. **Traction** — three numbers
3. **Team and ask** — who, how much, against what milestone

## Real pitch decks from real companies

These are publicly shared. Steal liberally.

### Pre-seed / Seed

- **Airbnb (2009 seed)** — The legendary 10-slide deck. [Pitch.com archive](https://pitch.com/templates/airbnb-pitch-deck-template). Note how blunt the "Problem" slide is.
- **Buffer (2011 seed)** — One of the first to publish their actual seed deck. Heavy on metrics.
- **YouTube (2005)** — Short, scrappy, 10 slides. Mostly about market timing.
- **Front (2016 seed)** — Strong "why now" slide. Good positioning argument.
- **MapMe (Seed)** — Founder Ben Lang published his deck and breakdown.
- **WeWork (early)** — Famous for visual storytelling, less for the company outcome.

### Series A / B

- **Linkedin (Series B, 2004)** — Reid Hoffman's annotated version is on his blog. Worth the deep read.
- **Mixpanel (Series B)** — Strong on engagement metrics framing.
- **Intercom (Series A)** — A textbook example of category positioning.
- **Square (Series B)** — Clean, visual, vision-led.

### Late-stage / Growth

- **Slack (Series F)** — Extreme focus on retention and engagement metrics, not feature count.
- **Notion (later rounds)** — Their decks lean heavily on user community and breadth of use cases.

### Famous "what not to do" decks

- **Theranos** — Master class in vague hand-waving. Read it as a cautionary tale.
- **Many WeWork era decks** — Heavy on ideology, light on unit economics.

## Pitch deck principles to internalize

### 1. "What" before "why"

The investor reading your deck for the first time has 90 seconds. If they can't answer "what does this company do?" by slide 2, the rest is wasted.

### 2. One idea per slide

If you're squeezing two ideas onto one slide, split it. The investor's eye finds one thing per slide. Two ideas = the second one disappears.

### 3. Show, don't tell

A 10-second demo gif beats a paragraph of text. A customer quote beats your own claim. A revenue chart beats the word "growing."

### 4. The deck is not the pitch

The deck supports a conversation. It is not a self-contained essay. If you're sending it cold, attach a Loom — 3 minutes of you walking through the slides converts 5x better than the deck alone.

### 5. End with the ask

Last slide: how much you're raising, against what milestones, and what you'd do with the money. Don't be coy.

### 6. Headlines, not titles

"Market" is a title. "$45B by 2028, growing 32% YoY" is a headline. Slide titles should be conclusions.

### 7. The team slide is for vibes

Investors decide on team in the first 10 minutes. Faces, names, one line each — what you've done before, why you're building this. No fluff.

### 8. Have a backup version

When an investor asks for a "1-pager," they want a shareable artifact. Always have:
- The full deck (12–18 slides)
- The 3-slide TL;DR
- A 1-page memo (PDF or doc, all-text)
- A 90-second Loom demo

Each one converts a different kind of investor.

## Red flags investors look for

- **Hockey-stick projections with no plan to get there** — be specific about the bridge from today to year 3.
- **TAM calculated bottom-up but unrealistic** — "We'll capture 1% of a $500B market" is a flag, not a number.
- **Team slide with placeholder photos / no LinkedIn links** — looks unfinished or hidden.
- **No mention of competition** — either you don't know the space, or you're hiding.
- **Vague differentiation ("we have better UX")** — show it, don't claim it.
- **Use of funds that's all "hire 10 engineers"** — what specifically gets built / launched / moved?
- **No metrics at all on a Series A deck** — instant skip.
